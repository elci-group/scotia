Tribunal preparation is not about rehearsing performance. It is about building a case so robust that performance becomes irrelevant.

At SeriousAboutSolutions, we have observed a recurring pattern among organisations facing employment tribunal claims. The panic sets in late. Documentation is scattered. Witnesses are unprepared. The narrative, if one exists at all, has been constructed reactively rather than strategically. By this stage, the organisation is not defending its position. It is managing decline.

This is entirely preventable.

The foundation of effective tribunal preparation lies in understanding what tribunals actually evaluate. They assess evidence, consistency, procedural fairness, and the credibility of documentation over time. A well-prepared case does not emerge from a flurry of activity in the weeks before a hearing. It is cultivated through disciplined case building from the moment a dispute becomes conceivable.

Let us address documentation first. Employment tribunals place significant weight on contemporaneous records. Emails drafted months after the fact carry less authority than dated, detailed notes taken at the time of incidents. We frequently encounter organisations with policies that exist only in theory, or meeting records so vague they could describe any conversation about any topic. Your documentation must tell a specific story with specific dates, specific decisions, and specific reasoning. Vagueness is not neutrality. It is vulnerability.

Witness preparation follows from this. Witnesses who understand the documentary evidence, who have reviewed their own prior statements, who can articulate the organisational rationale for decisions without defensiveness or embellishment, present credibly. Witnesses parachuted into preparation sessions days before a hearing often undermine cases they were intended to support. They contradict documents. They introduce explanations never previously offered. They appear, to the tribunal, as precisely what they are: unprepared.

Procedural fairness is the third pillar. Tribunals examine whether processes were followed, whether appeal rights were honoured, whether the Acas code was respected. Technical compliance matters. But so does the appearance of fairness. A process that was technically correct yet visibly rushed, or conducted by individuals with clear conflicts of interest, damages credibility. Case building includes auditing not merely what was done, but how it would appear to an independent observer.

Risk assessment must be honest. Organisations sometimes pursue tribunal defence on principle, underestimating cost, distraction, and reputational exposure. Strategic case building includes evaluating settlement at every stage, with clear-eyed analysis of strengths and weaknesses. Principle has its place. So does pragmatism.

The organisations that fare best are those that treat tribunal preparation as a discipline, not an emergency. They invest in training managers to document properly from the outset. They review policies before disputes arise. They engage specialists early enough to shape the narrative rather than merely respond to it.

Employment tribunal claims have risen significantly. The complexity of working arrangements, the heightened awareness of rights, the economic pressures on both employers and employees—all contribute to an environment where disputes are more likely. Preparation is not pessimism. It is operational maturity.

If your organisation has not reviewed its documentation practices, its management training, or its escalation protocols recently, the question is not whether you will face a claim. It is whether you will be ready when you do.
