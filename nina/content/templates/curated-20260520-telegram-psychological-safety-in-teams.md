Psychological safety isn't about being nice. It's about being real.

Teams that perform well share one trait: members speak up without fear of humiliation. No blame. No silent exits. Just direct, useful exchange.

Here's what that actually looks like:

→ "I made a mistake" is met with curiosity, not career damage
→ Disagreement happens in the room, not the corridor
→ Questions are signals of engagement, not incompetence

Without it? You get compliance theatre. People nod, then sabotage. Innovation dies. Talent leaves for competitors who listen better.

Building it takes deliberate work:

1. Leaders model vulnerability first
2. Responses to errors focus on systems, not scapegoats
3. Meetings include dissent before decisions close
4. Performance metrics reward contribution, not just agreement

Quick test: When did someone on your team last challenge you directly? If you can't remember, you don't have safety—you have silence.
