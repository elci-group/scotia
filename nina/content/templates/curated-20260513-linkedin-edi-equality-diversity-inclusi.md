Most organisations have now run the obligatory EDI training session. Tick-box complete. Certificate issued. Back to business as usual.

And that, frankly, is where the problem starts.

I have sat through enough of these sessions to recognise the pattern: a polished presentation, a few eye-opening statistics, perhaps an uncomfortable video clip, and a rush of well-meaning enthusiasm that evaporates by Tuesday. The intention is sound. The execution is often abysmal. What passes for EDI training in too many workplaces is little more than awareness-raising theatre—designed to demonstrate compliance rather than cultivate genuine cultural shift.

Here is what I have learned from observing hundreds of organisations grapple with this: EDI training that actually works looks nothing like the standard package.

First, it is never a one-off event. You cannot transform entrenched behaviours, unconscious bias, or systemic inequity in a ninety-minute webinar. Effective EDI development is embedded, iterative, and longitudinal. It requires follow-up, practice, and—crucially—accountability mechanisms that connect learning to lived workplace experience. Without this, your training investment is performative expenditure, not developmental spend.

Second, it must be organisation-specific. Generic off-the-shelf modules fail because they do not reflect your particular power dynamics, your industry context, your demographic composition, your historical grievances. The manufacturing firm with a predominantly male shop floor faces different challenges to the professional services partnership struggling with socioeconomic diversity. Tailored content, grounded in your actual organisational data and employee voice, will always outperform standardised content, however slickly produced.

Third, psychological safety matters enormously. Participants need space to ask awkward questions, to admit confusion, to surface resistance without immediate condemnation. Trainers who shut down difficult conversations in pursuit of a harmonious session may win the hour, but they lose the war. The discomfort needs to move from the training room into the organisational system, where real decisions about hiring, promotion, pay, and voice get made.

Fourth, leadership visibility is non-negotiable. When senior figures conspicuously absent themselves from EDI sessions, the message is unambiguous: this matters for you, not for us. The most effective programmes I have witnessed involve leaders not merely as attendees but as active participants—sharing their own developmental journeys, acknowledging their blind spots, modelling the vulnerability they wish to see normalised.

Fifth, and most critically, training must connect to structural action. If your EDI programme is not accompanied by transparent pay gap reporting, revised promotion criteria, diverse interview panels, flexible working as default, and robust challenge mechanisms for discriminatory behaviour, then you are essentially teaching people to recognise injustice without equipping them—or empowering them—to address it. That is a recipe for cynicism, not commitment.

At SeriousAboutSolutions, we design EDI interventions that integrate with broader organisational development: policy review, investigation protocols, mentoring architecture, and team dynamics. Because isolated training, however expertly delivered, rarely shifts the dial. Sustainable inclusion requires systemic intentionality.

The question for your organisation is not whether you have run EDI training. It is whether that training has altered who gets heard, who gets promoted, who gets to belong.

If the answer is uncertain, your work has barely begun.
