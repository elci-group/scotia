Neurodiversity at work isn't a niche topic. It's a business advantage you're probably underutilising.

The stats: 15-20% of the population is neurodivergent. ADHD, autism, dyslexia, dyspraxia—your team already includes these minds. The question is whether your workplace lets them perform or sets them up to struggle.

Common failures:
- Vague job descriptions that screen out literal thinkers
- Unstructured interviews that reward smooth talkers over skilled doers
- Open-plan offices designed for neurotypical stamina
- "Be more proactive" feedback with zero actionable detail

The fix? Specificity.

Clear instructions. Explicit expectations. Flexible environments. These benefit everyone, not just neurodivergent employees. It's universal design, not special treatment.

Recruitment: ditch the generic "excellent communication skills" requirement unless you've defined what that means for the role. Offer work trials alongside interviews. Some candidates build brilliant systems; they don't sell themselves in 30-minute conversations.

Retention: noise-cancelling headphones aren't expensive. Written agendas before meetings cost nothing. Advance notice of changes respects cognitive load.

Performance: manage outputs, not eye contact. A developer who delivers clean code without small talk isn't disengaged—they're efficient.

Legal angle: the Equality Act 2010 requires reasonable adjustments. Tribunals on neurodiversity claims are rising. "We didn't know" doesn't hold up when guidance is publicly available.

Team dynamics: neurodiverse teams solve problems faster. Different cognitive approaches mean fewer blind spots. McKinsey, Microsoft, SAP have proven this with dedicated hiring programmes.

Bottom line: inclusion isn't charity. It's competitive edge through cognitive diversity.
