Microlearning and just-in-time training are reshaping how organisations build capability without pulling people away from their work for days at a time. At SeriousAboutSolutions, we have seen a clear shift in what clients request, and it is worth examining why this approach is gaining traction and where it risks falling short.

The principle is straightforward. People need specific knowledge at the moment they face a relevant task or decision. Rather than completing a comprehensive twelve-month programme and hoping the right module surfaces from memory, employees access targeted content when the context demands it. A manager preparing for a difficult conversation pulls up a ten-minute guide on structured feedback. A team member encountering a new software feature watches a three-minute demonstration. The learning is immediate, applicable, and brief.

This aligns with how adults actually retain information. Cognitive load theory suggests we process and remember content better when it arrives in manageable chunks, particularly when connected to real situations. The forgetting curve is brutal; without reinforcement or immediate application, people lose most of what they learned within weeks. Just-in-time training interrupts this decay by collapsing the gap between learning and doing.

From an organisational perspective, the efficiency argument is compelling. Traditional training often involves travel, cover arrangements, and significant time away from productive work. Microlearning reduces this friction. It also allows organisations to respond faster to emerging needs. When legislation changes, when new systems launch, when a crisis demands swift upskilling, lengthy curriculum design is a luxury few can afford.

However, the enthusiasm for microlearning sometimes overlooks its limitations. Not every topic compresses into five minutes. Complex problem-solving, ethical reasoning, leadership judgement—these develop through extended practice, feedback, and reflection. A series of short videos will not build strategic thinking. Nor will it create the psychological safety that allows people to admit confusion and ask deeper questions. Some capabilities require immersion, not snippets.

There is also the risk of fragmentation. Without curation, employees accumulate disconnected tips rather than coherent understanding. They know a little about many things without grasping how pieces connect. Organisations must design pathways, not just content libraries. The architecture matters as much as the assets.

Another concern is measurement. Completion rates for microlearning modules often look impressive. Whether behaviour actually changes is harder to establish. If nobody assesses whether that difficult conversation guide improved management practice, the organisation is merely distributing content, not developing people.

At SeriousAboutSolutions, we integrate microlearning into broader strategies rather than treating it as a replacement. For tribunal case preparation, the detail demands thoroughness no brief module can provide. For mentoring relationships, the value lies in sustained dialogue, not isolated interventions. For team building, trust builds through shared experience over time, not through quick hits.

The most effective implementations we see combine approaches. Microlearning handles updates, refreshers, and immediate procedural needs. Deeper interventions address transformation, culture, and complex skill development. The choice depends on what the organisation genuinely needs to achieve.
