The best managers I know spend less time telling people what to do and more time helping them figure it out themselves. That shift—from directive to developmental—is the core of coaching, and it is not a soft skill. It is a performance multiplier.

Most managers default to solving problems because it feels efficient. Someone brings you an issue, you offer the fix, they leave, and the immediate pressure drops. But here is what happens next: they return with the same problem, slightly repackaged, because they never built the capability to resolve it independently. You become the bottleneck, and they stay stuck.

Coaching breaks that cycle. It requires managers to tolerate discomfort—their own, mostly—while someone else thinks aloud, stumbles, and eventually lands on a solution. It takes longer in the moment. It saves hours downstream.

So what does this look like in practice?

Start with genuine curiosity. Not the performative kind where you already know the answer and are guiding someone toward it like a rigged quiz. I mean real curiosity about how they see the situation, what they have already tried, and what they are actually trying to achieve. The goal is understanding, not triage.

Ask questions that open up thinking rather than narrow it. "What have you considered so far?" beats "Have you tried X?" The first invites exploration. The second implies there is one correct path—and that you hold the map.

Listen for what is not being said. Hesitation often signals risk aversion or political sensitivity. Overconfidence can mask uncertainty. A manager with coaching skills notices these gaps and gently surfaces them without interrogation. "You seem certain about this approach—what would need to be true for it to fail?" That question tests assumptions without attacking them.

Resist the rescue impulse. When you see the obvious flaw in someone's reasoning, the urge to correct it is physical. Wait. Let them arrive there, or ask a question that helps them see the gap themselves. Ownership of the solution drives ownership of the outcome. Handed-down answers generate compliance at best.

Finally, agree on clear accountability. Coaching is not therapy or endless conversation. It should end with specific actions, timelines, and check-ins. The employee leaves with a plan they shaped, and you leave with confidence they can execute it.

The organisations we work with at SeriousAboutSolutions often ask us to build these capabilities at scale—usually after realising their most technically brilliant managers are accidentally disempowering their teams. We design practical programmes that move managers from knowing this conceptually to doing it habitually, because awareness without application is just trivia.

If your managers are still the smartest people in every room, you have a ceiling problem. Coaching is how you raise it.
